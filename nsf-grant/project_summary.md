# Project Summary

## Overview

Rehabilitation and physical-training decisions depend on muscle-level state — force production, fatigue onset, deep-muscle activation, and hidden compensation patterns — that no current non-invasive wearable or vision system can observe. As a result, state-of-the-art physical-activity AI, from smartphone-video biomechanics and skeleton-based rehabilitation scoring to large-scale surface-electromyography interfaces, is structurally limited to external kinematics and can confuse compensatory motion with genuine recovery. Wearable ultrasound is the only non-invasive modality that directly images internal muscle architecture, activation, and mechanics during movement, yet it is absent from every current multimodal foundation model and physical-activity benchmark.

**Objectives and approach.** This four-year program will (i) build a large-scale paired dataset of synchronized wearable ultrasound, surface EMG, and video during structured human movement; (ii) develop and openly release the first ultrasound-aware multimodal foundation model that aligns tissue-level signals with vision and language; and (iii) validate the framework against clinician-rated outcomes in a longitudinal post-stroke upper-limb rehabilitation study. **Aim 1** establishes the dataset during structured rehabilitation and training protocols, annotated with clinician-rated quality scores and standardized functional-outcome measures. **Aim 2** develops a self-supervised ultrasound encoder and aligns it with vision-language representations through CLIP-style contrastive training. **Aim 3** constructs a clinical benchmark — covering compensation detection, fatigue onset, movement-quality scoring, and functional-outcome regression — and evaluates the framework in partnership with a rehabilitation clinic. Success criteria include open release of the dataset, encoder, and benchmark under permissive licenses, and demonstrable improvement in clinical-scale prediction over vision-only and EMG-only baselines, measured against clinician assessments.

## Intellectual Merit

The project advances knowledge at three intersections. First, it introduces *internal-body observables* as a first-class input to multimodal reasoning, directly addressing documented shortfalls of vision-language models on physical-world understanding — a limitation that recent evaluations suggest does not close with model or data scale alone. Second, it develops representation-learning methods for wearable ultrasound, a temporally dense, operator-dependent, anatomy-variant modality without a pretrained backbone, extending multimodal alignment techniques beyond the current vision-language-touch regime. Third, it produces the earliest systematic paired musculoskeletal dataset and clinical benchmark linking tissue-level signals to validated outcome measures, establishing a rigorous basis for evaluating rehabilitation AI. Together, these artifacts close a gap that no single community — ultrasound, EMG, vision, or clinical rehabilitation — has been positioned to close alone.

## Broader Impacts

The project will deliver open research infrastructure for an underserved area: a de-identified multimodal dataset, a pretrained ultrasound encoder, and a public clinical benchmark, all released under permissive licenses. In practice, objective muscle-level feedback has the potential to improve rehabilitation outcomes for post-stroke, post-surgical, and older-adult populations who disproportionately lack access to frequent specialist supervision. The project will train graduate and undergraduate researchers at the intersection of machine learning, biomedical engineering, and clinical science, with structured recruitment through programs that serve students underrepresented in computing. A formal partnership with a rehabilitation clinic will enable clinician-in-the-loop model evaluation and translation, and K–12 engagement through a local adaptive-sports partner will introduce students to biomedical AI via demonstrations of wearable sensing and real-time movement analysis. Impact will be assessed through dataset and model download counts, benchmark submissions and citations, clinical-partner adoption, and trainee publication and career outcomes.
